by Charles Victor
Design can mobilize its forces away from subjective creativity and private authorship, and instead favor the development of systems toward cultural commons.
“Some of us have lent our expertise to initiatives that abuse the law and human rights, defeat critical systems of encryption and privacy, and put lives at risk.” (First Things First 2014)
“Some of us have lent our expertise to initiatives that abuse the law and human rights, defeat critical systems of encryption and privacy, and put lives at risk.” (First Things First 2014) We as designers have sold out. Our goal should not be to design without caution, scrutiny, or responsibility. It is our responsibility as designers, even more so as humans, to be aware and critical of our effect on the world. Technology isn’t necessarily the enemy, it effects are the byproduct of institutional greed, bias, and unfocused design. Technology is a tool, not a pervasive entity. We as humans should then take responsibility for our actions with this tool, and design with scrutiny and constraint. This approach may deviate slightly from the traditionally contrived human-centered approach, and instead be something of a humanity-centered approach – one with community as a larger picture more broadly defined and respected. This is the key to building a world that is inclusive, and it is a step in the right direction for designing a better future. Designers should take time and reflect on their own core beliefs before committing to another’s. Upon defining your own values, refer back to these: Trust, Transparency, Liberty, Responsibility, and Action.
1. Trust
Trust–in your design, your client, your peers, your audience, and yourself. More so than your trust towards another, it is absolutely necessary to have them trust you. The only way to build that trust is through your ability as a designer to communicate your intention, with honesty and transparency. Never should you break that trust with another, unless they have infringed upon your trust first.
2. Transparency
Transparency–is the key to most of the other principles presented here. Without it, you are enigmatic; something no one wishes to deal with when working with another. Your actions and intention should remain on the surface of your work, and you should never deviate from your core beliefs. Transparency is the key to remaining an honest individual; one that holds no secrets, so that the community you surround yourself with can expect only the best of you.
3. Liberty
Liberty–is the key to a society of equity and equality. Never should someone feel oppressed in any system, so you must design with the intention of inclusivity. If your design infringes on key aspects of liberty, such as privacy, you are perpetuating an oppressive system in which the people are treated as commodities. This would directly contradict your desire for a humanity-centered approach to world building.
4. Responsibility
Responsibility-to your design, your client, your peers, your audience, and yourself. Without it, you are apathetic; something no one wishes to ever deal with. It is imperative that we as designers are aware of the impact our work has on the world. You must take accountability seriously if you ever wish to fix corruption and oppression. Responsibility extends beyond your own actions; we must begin to take responsibility for the actions of humanity as a whole: such as is the case with climate change.
5. Action
Action–is necessary if anything were to ever get done. Do not just preach; you must also practice.

“We can’t separate who we are as people from who we are as designers. We teach what we preach, we practice what we preach. Designers are humans first.”
